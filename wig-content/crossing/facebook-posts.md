## WEEK 1 — The Door

**Week 1, Post 1**
The hardest part of walking out isn't what's behind you. It's that nobody told you what's in front of you. That's why this exists.

*Image direction: Dark background, single light source. Text overlay: "Nobody told you what's in front of you." Clean, minimal.*

---

**Week 1, Post 2**
You survived something that breaks most people. The question now isn't whether you're strong enough. It's whether you have the right information. Strength without a map is just suffering in a different place.

*Image direction: Open road, morning light, shot from the driver's seat or walking perspective. Conveys forward motion.*

---

**Week 1, Post 3**
Get your ID first. Before the job search. Before the apartment hunt. Before anything else. Birth certificate, Social Security card, state ID — in that order. Everything else in reentry runs through that paper. Start there.

*Image direction: Clean flat lay of documents on a desk. No faces. Practical, organized, calm.*

## WEEK 2 — What Nobody Says Out Loud

**Week 2, Post 1**
Nobody talks about the first night. The relief and the terror sitting right next to each other. The sounds that aren't there anymore. The silence that doesn't feel like peace yet. You're not broken. You're adjusting. Keep going.

*Image direction: Window with late evening light. Simple, quiet. Someone looking out.*

---

**Week 2, Post 2**
The system will tell you what you can't have. It rarely tells you what you can. You can get SNAP. You may qualify for Medicaid. You can open a bank account. You can apply for housing. Start applying before you think you're ready.

*Image direction: Checklist graphic with items being checked off. Bold, clean design. Empowering.*

---

**Week 2, Post 3**
Your family waited. That doesn't mean coming home is easy. Trust is rebuilt in actions, not in the moment you walk through the door. Show up consistently for 30 days before you say anything about what you need.

*Image direction: Two pairs of hands — not romantic, familial. Warm light. Simple.*

## WEEK 3 — The Employment Reality

**Week 3, Post 1**
The question is coming: "Have you ever been convicted of a felony?" Here's what works: answer it before they ask. Three sentences. What happened. What changed. What you're doing now. Own it. Don't perform it.

*Image direction: Person in interview posture — seated, composed, confident. Professional setting.*

---

**Week 3, Post 2**
JPMorgan Chase. Walmart. Home Depot. Dave's Hot Chicken. Greyston Bakery. These companies have made public commitments to hire people with records. They're not doing you a favor. You're qualified. Find the door that's already open.

*Image direction: Professional environment, someone at work. Clean, aspirational without being unrealistic.*

---

**Week 3, Post 3**
A certification changes your conversation. OSHA 10. ServSafe. CDL. CompTIA. You can get most of these in 30-90 days. Some are free through workforce development. None of them care about your record. The credential speaks first.

*Image direction: Certification document or training environment. Practical, not glamorized.*

## WEEK 4 — What You're Building

**Week 4, Post 1**
Reentry is not a second chance at your old life. It's a first chance at a different one. The sooner you stop trying to recover what you had, the sooner you can build what you need.

*Image direction: Building materials or construction metaphor. Brick, foundation, something being built from the ground up.*

---

**Week 4, Post 2**
If you're reading this and you're still inside — start now. Research what reentry organizations are in the city you're going back to. Know the resources before you need them. Preparation is the only thing you can control right now.

*Image direction: Person writing in a notebook. Purposeful energy. Could be anywhere.*

---

**Week 4, Post 3**
The 30-day mark is not arrival. It's the end of the beginning. By day 30 you should have: ID, a benefits application in, an employment plan, a stable place to sleep, and one person in your life who will tell you the truth. That's the foundation.

*Image direction: Simple checklist graphic. Clean. Calm. Numbered.*

## WEEK 5 — For the Families

**Week 5, Post 1**
For everyone waiting on someone to come home: what they need most in the first 30 days is not your opinion about what they should have done. It's a place to sleep and someone who's genuinely glad they're out. Start there.

*Image direction: Front door from the outside. Late afternoon light. Quiet anticipation.*

---

**Week 5, Post 2**
Children don't need a perfect explanation of where their parent was. They need to see that parent show up consistently now. Every pickup. Every call. Every dinner. Presence over explanation. Every time.

*Image direction: Parent and child, not posed — a genuine moment. Walking together, playing, something ordinary.*

---

**Week 5, Post 3**
Loving someone through reentry requires you to hold two things at once: what they're capable of and what they actually are right now. Give them room to grow into who they're becoming. Don't demand the finished version on Day 1.

*Image direction: Two people walking side by side on a path. Equal, forward-facing. Not one leading the other.*

## WEEK 6 — The Longer View

**Week 6, Post 1**
The system counted on you failing. Every obstacle in reentry was designed — consciously or not — to make it harder to stay out than to go back. Knowing that doesn't make you a victim. It makes you prepared for the real game.

*Image direction: Person standing at the edge of something large — a city, a field, a horizon. Scale and possibility.*

---

**Week 6, Post 2**
The Inmate Traveler's Guide started as notes I wrote for myself. Then for people I knew. Then it became a book. Not because I figured everything out — because I figured out enough to pass it on. That's what this is.

*Image direction: Book cover or book being held. Authentic, not staged. Human.*

**Week 6, Post 3**
You don't need to forgive the system to move through it. You don't need to be grateful for surviving something you shouldn't have had to survive. You just need to know where the next door is. We'll keep pointing at it.

*Image direction: Open door, light beyond it. Symbolic but not cliché — find an angle that feels honest.*
