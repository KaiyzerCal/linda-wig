**Body:**

Three weeks after I got out, I was sitting in a parking lot.

I had the address of a job. I had the time of the interview. I had pressed the one decent shirt I owned. And I could not make myself get out of the car.

Not because I didn't want the job. I wanted it badly enough that I'd practiced the disclosure conversation forty times in the mirror the night before. But sitting there in that parking lot, something else was happening. Something I didn't have a name for at the time.

## EMAIL 3 — Practical Value

**Subject:** The background check thing nobody explains

**Preview text:** You have more rights than you think. Here's what they are.

**Body:**

Here's something most people navigating reentry don't know:

Background check companies make mistakes. Regularly.

Items that should have been expunged still show up. Arrests without convictions appear as convictions. Records from one state bleed into reports from another. Old information gets misattributed to people with similar names.

And here's the thing — you have a legal right to dispute every single error.

The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) governs background check companies the same way it governs credit bureaus. If a company provides an inaccurate report to a potential employer, and that inaccuracy costs you a job, you have legal standing to dispute the report and potentially pursue damages.

Here's what to do:

**Step 1:** Get a copy of your background check. If an employer runs one and denies you because of it, they are required by law to tell you which company ran it.

**Step 2:** Request your report from that company directly. Most allow one free copy per year.

**Step 3:** Identify every error. Write them down with specific dates and case numbers.

**Step 4:** Submit a written dispute to the background check company with documentation supporting the correction.

**Step 5:** If the error is significant and caused you to lose a job, contact a consumer rights attorney. Many take FCRA cases on contingency — meaning you pay nothing unless you win.

This won't fix a legitimate record. But it will fix an inaccurate one — and more records have errors than you'd expect.

More practical intelligence next time. Stay on the list.

— Bishop
