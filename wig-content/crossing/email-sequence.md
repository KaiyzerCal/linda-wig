**Email 2 — The One Thing Most People Skip**
Subject: Most people skip this and pay for it later
Preview text: ID first. Everything else depends on it.

---

[Name],

I want to talk about the one step most people skip in the first week.

Not because they don't know they need it. But because it feels like a small errand compared to everything else happening at once. So they push it.

Then Week 3 arrives and they still can't open a bank account. Can't complete a job application. Can't sign a lease.

The step: identification.

Birth certificate → Social Security card → State ID. In that order. The first unlocks the second. The second unlocks the third. Together they unlock everything else.

Birth certificate: Request online from your state of birth. $10-25. Look for reentry fee waivers if you can't cover it.

Social Security Administration: Bring your birth certificate. Free. Takes about 2 weeks to arrive.

State DMV: Bring both documents. Some states have waived fees for people in reentry. Ask.

If you're working with a reentry organization, many of them will walk you through this for free. If you're not connected to one yet, go to 211.org and type in your zip code.

This is not complicated. But it has to be first.

— Bishop

**Email 3 — The Conversation About Work**
Subject: They're going to ask. Here's what to say.
Preview text: Own it before they find it.

---

[Name],

Every job application eventually gets to the question.

"Have you ever been convicted of a felony?"

I spent a long time dreading that question. Then I figured out that the dread was costing me more than the record was.

Here's what works:

Answer it before they ask. Not in a confessional way. Not as a performance. Three sentences:

1. What happened — stated plainly, without minimizing or dramatizing
2. What changed — what you learned, what you did, what you decided
3. What you're doing now — where your focus is, what you're building

That's it. You're not asking for sympathy. You're giving them information and letting them see a person who owns their story rather than hiding from it.

Some employers will still say no. That's real. But many won't. And you need to be in front of enough of them that the ones who will say yes actually get the chance to.

Companies with public second-chance hiring commitments include JPMorgan Chase, Walmart, Home Depot, McDonald's, and hundreds of others. The Second Chance Business Coalition has a full list.

Find the open doors. Walk through them.

— Bishop

**Email 4 — What the System Won't Say**
Subject: What nobody tells you about reentry benefits
Preview text: You may qualify for more than you think.

---

[Name],

I want to address something people get wrong.

Most people coming home assume their conviction disqualifies them from benefits. In many cases, that's not true.

SNAP (food assistance): Most people with convictions qualify. The exception is drug felony convictions in states that still enforce that ban — but many states have removed that restriction. Apply immediately. Processing takes 30 days.

Medicaid: Low or no income qualifies you. Incarceration doesn't disqualify you. Mental health and substance use treatment is covered. Apply through your state's marketplace.

Pell Grants: If you're thinking about school, you may qualify. The federal ban on Pell Grants for people with convictions was largely lifted in 2023. This is a real door now.

Veteran? Contact the VA. A felony conviction does not automatically disqualify you from VA benefits.

None of this is free money falling from the sky. It's a system you paid into and a system you have rights within.

Apply for everything. Let them deny you if they're going to. But don't disqualify yourself before you even apply.

— Bishop
