# The First 30 Days: A Reentry Survival Checklist
### By Bishop Christopher Watkins

---

*This guide is for you. Not for the caseworker. Not for the parole officer. For you. I wrote it because nobody handed me anything like this when I walked out, and I spent the first two weeks making mistakes that cost me months. You don't have that kind of time to waste. Let's get to work.*

---

## Before You Read Another Word

You are out. Whatever that took — however long it took — you are out. That matters. But the first 30 days will test you in ways the inside never did, because out here the pressure is invisible. Nobody locks the door. Nobody tells you when to eat. The structure is gone, and for a lot of people, that absence of structure is the thing that sends them back.

This checklist exists to give you structure when the system won't give it to you.

Work through it in order. Don't skip sections because you think you've got it handled. The things people think they've got handled are usually the things that blow up on day 14.

---

## SECTION 1: HOUSING

### The First 72 Hours

Where you sleep tonight is the most important question you have right now. Everything else — employment, documents, benefits — depends on having a stable address. Without an address, you cannot get a state ID. Without a state ID, you cannot get a job. Without a job, you cannot pay rent. It all starts here.

**If you have a parole or supervised release condition requiring approved housing:**
- You must report to your assigned address. This is not optional.
- If that address has fallen through, call your PO before you do anything else. Do not show up without approved housing. Do not try to figure it out yourself first. Call.
- Most POs have emergency protocols. Use them. That's what they're for.

**If you have family or friends you can stay with:**
- Confirm the address is approved by your supervision officer before release if possible.
- Understand that some housing — Section 8, public housing — prohibits people with felony convictions from residing there. Staying without disclosure puts your host at risk of losing their housing. Have the conversation.
- A couch is not a plan. It's a starting point. Give yourself 30 days to find something more stable.

**Emergency Shelter Options:**
- Search 211.org or call 2-1-1 from any phone — this is the national social services line. It is free. It works. Tell them you are recently released and need emergency housing.
- The Salvation Army, Catholic Charities, and local mission-based shelters accept people with records in most cities. Call ahead. Beds go fast.
- Many cities have reentry-specific transitional housing — these are better options because the staff understand your situation. Ask your PO, your public defender's office, or any reentry nonprofit in your area.

**Transitional Housing Programs:**
Transitional housing gives you a bed, sometimes meals, and usually structure — curfews, meetings, job search requirements. This is not a step down. This is a bridge. Some of the most productive people I know used transitional housing to rebuild their foundation. It is not permanent. It is strategic.

- Length of stay varies: 30 days to 2 years depending on program
- Most programs require sobriety and program participation
- Many have job placement assistance built in
- Some are faith-based — if that's not your thing, there are secular options. Ask.

### Housing Checklist
- [ ] Confirmed approved address with supervision officer
- [ ] Called 2-1-1 if no stable housing yet
- [ ] Identified 2 backup options (shelter or transitional housing)
- [ ] Verified that host family/friend is not in jeopardy due to your record
- [ ] Have a 30-day housing plan in writing

## SECTION 2: ID RESTORATION

### Why This Is Your Highest Priority

You cannot get a job without ID. You cannot open a bank account. You cannot apply for benefits. You cannot rent an apartment. You need your documents, and you need them fast.

Here is the order of operations:

**Step 1: Social Security Card**
This is usually the easiest and cheapest to get first, and many other agencies require it.
- Go to ssa.gov or find your local Social Security Administration office
- Bring: proof of identity (prison release papers work in most states), proof of citizenship (birth certificate if you have it, but not always required for replacement card)
- It is free
- Allow 2-3 weeks for card to arrive by mail
- You are allowed 3 replacement cards per year, 10 in a lifetime

**Step 2: Birth Certificate**
- Order from the vital records office of the state where you were born — not where you live now
- Cost: $10–$25 depending on state
- Many states allow online ordering at vitalchek.com
- Turnaround: 2-6 weeks by mail; same-day or next-day in person at the county clerk's office
- If you cannot afford it: many reentry nonprofits and legal aid organizations will cover this cost. Ask.

**Step 3: State ID or Driver's License**
- Go to your state's DMV with: birth certificate, Social Security card, and proof of address (a piece of mail, a utility bill, a bank statement — something with your name and current address)
- Cost: $10–$35 depending on state
- If your license was suspended for non-payment of fines: many states now have programs to clear suspensions for people in financial hardship. Ask the DMV clerk specifically about hardship reinstatement before you pay anything.
- Some states issue IDs automatically upon release — ask your facility before you leave

**Step 4: Voter Registration**
- In most states, your voting rights are restored upon release from incarceration (some states require completion of parole)
- Register at vote.org — takes 2 minutes
- This matters. Register.

### Documents Checklist
- [ ] Social Security card ordered or received
- [ ] Birth certificate ordered or received
- [ ] State ID application submitted
- [ ] Proof of address document secured
- [ ] Voter registration completed

## SECTION 3: EMPLOYMENT

### The First Truth

Getting a job with a record is harder than they told you it would be when you were inside. It is also more possible than you think right now. The difference between the people who find work and the people who don't is not luck — it's approach.

**Where to Start — Day 1:**

Do not apply to Amazon and expect a callback. Do not apply online to large corporations with automated screening software that filters on felony convictions before a human ever sees your name. Start where the door is actually open.

**Temp agencies first.**
Labor-ready, Manpower, Adecco — these agencies place people daily. Many do not run background checks, or run them after placement. Show up in person. Dress clean. Be early. Say you are looking for immediate work. You can be earning income within 48 hours. This is not your career. This is your bridge.

**Second-chance employers.**
The Fair Chance Business Pledge is a list of companies that have committed to fair hiring. Search it. Companies like Dave's Hot Chicken, Greyston Bakery, and many local businesses have explicit second-chance hiring policies. Target and Home Depot have also made commitments — search "second chance employers [your city]" and you will find a list.

**The disclosure question.**
This is the one people get wrong most consistently. Here is what I know from experience and from working with hundreds of people post-release:

- Disclose before they find out. Every time.
- Brief, direct, and forward-looking: "I have a [felony/misdemeanor] on my record from [X years ago]. I served my time, I've been focused on rebuilding, and I'm ready to demonstrate my reliability."
- Practice this. Say it out loud 10 times. The discomfort goes away with repetition. The fumble in the interview does not.
- Never lie on an application. The background check will find it. The lie is worse than the record.

**Ban the Box Laws:**
Many cities and states now prohibit employers from asking about criminal history on the initial application. Know your state's law. If you're in a Ban the Box jurisdiction, they cannot legally ask until a conditional offer has been made. Search "[your state] ban the box law" — know your rights before you walk in.

**Workforce Development:**
Every state has a workforce development board or American Job Center. These are free. They offer resume help, interview coaching, skills training, and job placement. Some have specific reentry programs. Find yours at careeronestop.org.

### Employment Checklist
- [ ] Registered with 2 local temp agencies
- [ ] Researched second-chance employers in my city
- [ ] Prepared and practiced my disclosure statement
- [ ] Located nearest American Job Center
- [ ] Resume drafted (even a basic one — the Job Center can help)
- [ ] Dress clothes identified or secured for interviews

## SECTION 4: BENEFITS

### What You Qualify For Immediately

Do not let pride stop you from using what is available. These programs exist. You paid into them. Use them to stabilize while you build.

**SNAP (Food Assistance):**
- Most formerly incarcerated people qualify for SNAP immediately upon release
- Exception: if you have a drug trafficking conviction, some states restrict SNAP — check your state's specific rules
- Apply online at benefits.gov or in person at your local SNAP office
- You can receive benefits within 7 days if you meet expedited criteria (very low income, no resources)
- Average benefit: $200–$400/month depending on household size

**Medicaid:**
- If your income is below 138% of the federal poverty level and your state expanded Medicaid (most did), you qualify
- Apply at healthcare.gov or your state's Medicaid office
- Coverage begins quickly — often the same month you apply
- This covers mental health treatment and substance use treatment. Use it if you need it. That is not weakness. That is strategy.

**SSI/SSDI:**
- If you have a disability that prevents substantial employment, you may qualify for Supplemental Security Income (SSI)
- This takes time — often 3-6 months to process, and appeals are common
- Apply early. Apply even if you're not sure. The worst they say is no, and you can reapply.

**What You Cannot Get:**
- Federal student loans (Pell Grants) — available to most returning citizens now (rules changed in 2021)
- HUD public housing — each housing authority makes its own rules; some ban felony convictions, some do not. Check with your local housing authority directly.

**Reentry-Specific Assistance:**
Many cities and counties have reentry funds — small grants, clothing assistance, transportation help, phone assistance. Ask your PO, your reentry nonprofit, or call 2-1-1. These resources are underused because people don't know to ask.

### Benefits Checklist
- [ ] SNAP application submitted or scheduled
- [ ] Medicaid application submitted
- [ ] Contacted 2-1-1 about local reentry assistance programs
- [ ] Researched local workforce development benefits
- [ ] SSI/SSDI evaluated (if applicable)

## SECTION 5: MINDSET — THE HARDEST SECTION

### The First 72 Hours Will Lie to You

I need you to understand something that nobody in the system told me when I got out: freedom is disorienting. Not because you don't want it. You've wanted nothing else. But the absence of structure — the noise, the choices, the pace of everything on the outside — that absence will feel like anxiety. Like something is wrong.

Nothing is wrong. This is adjustment. It passes.

**What to expect from yourself:**

*Overwhelm.* The grocery store will have 40 kinds of cereal. The phone you're trying to figure out will have a thousand apps. People will be moving fast in every direction. You will want to go somewhere quiet and sit still. Do it. That's healthy.

*Hypervigilance.* You've spent years reading every room for threat. That doesn't turn off. You will feel it in restaurants, on public transit, in stores. You are not broken. Your nervous system is recalibrating. Give it 60 to 90 days.

*The pull toward what's familiar.* The people, places, and patterns that were there before you went in will feel like gravity. Some of that gravity is home. Some of it is a trap. You know which is which. Be honest with yourself.

*Loneliness.* Even surrounded by people, you may feel profoundly alone. The people who love you don't fully understand what you came through. That gap is real. Find community — a reentry support group, a faith community, a program — where the gap doesn't exist. People who've been where you've been do not have to explain themselves to each other.

**The one discipline that changes everything:**

Routine. Not because it's comfortable — you've had enough forced routine. But because a self-imposed routine is the first act of self-governance. It is you telling yourself: I decide how this day goes. Wake up at the same time. Eat. Move. Have a plan for the day before the day starts. This single practice is the difference between drifting and building.

**Who to tell about your record:**

Not everyone. You are not required to disclose your incarceration in casual conversation. You are not your record. You are the person who survived it and is now building something on the other side of it. Tell people who need to know — employers, housing providers, people who will be affected by it. Everyone else gets to know the version of you that exists right now.

**The question that matters:**

Not "how do I get back to who I was before?" You cannot go back. You are different now — shaped by time you cannot undo. The question is: who do I choose to be with what I know now, in the life I have in front of me?

That question, answered honestly and acted on daily, is how people rebuild.

### Mindset Checklist
- [ ] Identified one person I can call when I'm struggling (not to fix it — just to talk)
- [ ] Located a reentry support group or community in my area
- [ ] Written out a basic daily routine for the first week
- [ ] Named the three people / places / patterns I need distance from
- [ ] Named the one thing I am building toward — even if it's small
