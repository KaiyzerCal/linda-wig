# The First 30 Days: A Reentry Survival Checklist
### By Bishop Watkins | The Crossing Agency

---

> *The first 30 days will either set you up or set you back. This checklist exists so you go into them with a plan — not a prayer.*

---

## Before You Read This

I wrote this from the inside out. Not from a policy manual, not from a government website, not from someone who has never been where you've been.

The first 30 days after release are the most dangerous stretch of your reentry. Not because something bad is going to happen to you — but because everything is happening at once, and most people have no roadmap. They're running on adrenaline and relief, and then reality hits.

This checklist won't solve everything. But it will give you a sequence. And a sequence is how you survive when everything feels urgent at the same time.

Work through this in order. Check each box. Move to the next.

---

## WEEK 1 — Foundation

### Day 1-2: Reporting and Immediate Safety

- [ ] Report to your parole or probation officer within the required window (know this date before you walk out)
- [ ] Confirm your reporting address is locked in — do not be homeless on paper
- [ ] Identify where you will sleep tonight, tomorrow, and the rest of week one
- [ ] Call any family member or support person you made arrangements with before release
- [ ] Do not go to places, people, or situations connected to your case — not yet, not this week

**The honest truth:** The first night is emotional. It's supposed to be. But Day 2 starts your clock. Get moving.

---

### Day 3-5: Identification — Your First Priority

Without ID, nothing else works. No bank account. No job application. No benefits. No apartment. ID is your entry point into everything.

- [ ] **Birth Certificate** — Request from the vital records office in your state of birth. Most states allow online requests. Cost: $10-$25. If you can't afford it, ask your PO or reentry org about fee waivers.
- [ ] **Social Security Card** — Go to your local Social Security Administration office with your birth certificate and any other ID you have. Free. Can take 2 weeks.
- [ ] **State ID or Driver's License** — Go to your state DMV with birth certificate + Social Security card. Cost varies by state. Some states have reentry fee waivers — ask.
- [ ] **Check if your license was suspended** — If it was, find out what's required to reinstate before you drive anything.

**Shortcuts:** Many reentry organizations will help you get these documents faster and for free. Find the reentry nonprofit in your city before you need it.

---

### Day 5-7: Safe Housing Confirmed

- [ ] Confirm your Week 2 and Week 3 housing plan — don't just assume where you landed is stable
- [ ] If staying with family, have a direct conversation about expectations and timeline
- [ ] If in a halfway house or transitional program, read the rules before you violate one by accident
- [ ] Research Section 8 / HUD housing — get on the waitlist now, even if it's years out
- [ ] Ask your reentry case manager or PO about emergency housing resources in your area

**What they don't tell you:** The housing you have the first week may not be available in week three. Start planning your next move before you need to.

---

## WEEK 2 — Identity and Benefits

### Benefits You May Qualify For

Many people don't apply for benefits because they assume a conviction disqualifies them. That is often wrong. Check each one.

- [ ] **SNAP (Food Stamps)** — Apply at your local Department of Social Services. Most people with convictions qualify unless it's a drug felony in a state that still enforces that ban. Apply immediately — it can take 30 days to process.
- [ ] **Medicaid / Health Coverage** — If you have no income or low income, apply through your state's Medicaid office or Healthcare.gov. Mental health and substance use treatment coverage is critical early in reentry.
- [ ] **SSI/SSDI** — If you have a disability or mental health condition, you may qualify for Supplemental Security Income. The application process is long — start now.
- [ ] **Veterans Benefits** — If you served, contact the VA. A felony conviction does not automatically disqualify you from VA benefits.
- [ ] **Unemployment** — In most states, you cannot collect unemployment if you were incarcerated during your benefit period. But check your state's rules — some exceptions apply.

---

### Banking

- [ ] Open a bank account or prepaid debit account — you need somewhere for any income to land
- [ ] If banks have denied you due to ChexSystems, look for "second chance" checking accounts at credit unions
- [ ] Do not keep cash you can't account for

---

## WEEK 3 — Employment

### Before You Apply Anywhere

- [ ] Know exactly what your conviction record looks like — order a background check on yourself
- [ ] Know which convictions will show up and which won't (some states seal or limit certain records — research yours)
- [ ] Know your state's "Ban the Box" laws — many employers cannot ask about convictions on the initial application
- [ ] Prepare a one-paragraph honest explanation of your conviction that you can say without shame and without oversharing

---

### Finding Work

- [ ] Contact your state's American Job Center (workforce development office) — they have reentry employment specialists
- [ ] Ask your reentry organization for employer referrals — they know who actually hires
- [ ] Look at companies with Second Chance hiring commitments: Dave's Hot Chicken, Greyston Bakery, Homeboy Industries, and many national companies including JPMorgan Chase, Walmart, and Home Depot have public second-chance hiring pledges
- [ ] Consider certification programs — OSHA 10, forklift certification, ServSafe (food handler), CompTIA IT certifications — many are short, affordable, and open to people with convictions
- [ ] Do not lie on a job application. An honest record you own is survivable. A lie that gets discovered is not.

---

### If No Job Is Coming Quickly

- [ ] Day labor is not a plan, but it can bridge a gap — find the day labor centers near you
- [ ] Gig work (DoorDash, Uber, TaskRabbit) can work in the short term — most don't run background checks or run limited ones
- [ ] Tell your PO what's happening — a PO who knows you're trying is more likely to work with you

---

## WEEK 4 — Stability and Forward Motion

### Legal Obligations

- [ ] Know every condition of your supervision — violations happen when people assume, not when they're informed
- [ ] Know your travel restrictions — can you cross county lines? State lines? What do you need permission for?
- [ ] If you're paying fines, fees, or restitution, make a payment plan and document it
- [ ] Know your next court date, reporting date, or review date

---

### Mental Health and Mindset

This is not optional. The adjustment from inside to outside is harder than most people admit.

- [ ] Connect with a counselor, therapist, or peer support group — ask your PO or reentry org to refer you
- [ ] Identify two people in your life who are honest with you and not connected to your old situation
- [ ] Make a list of triggers — situations, places, people — and have a plan for each one
- [ ] Decide what you are building toward, not just what you're running from

**The hardest truth:** You can do everything right and still have hard days. That doesn't mean you're failing. It means you're adjusting. Keep moving.

---

### Building the Next 60 Days

- [ ] Set one employment goal for the next 30 days
- [ ] Set one housing stability goal for the next 60 days
- [ ] Identify one skill or certification you want to pursue
- [ ] Schedule your next check-in with your case manager, PO, or support person

---

## Resources to Find In Your Area

- **211.org** — Dial 2-1-1 or visit the site. Local resources for food, housing, legal aid, and more.
- **reentrycouncil.org** — National Reentry Resource Center
- **Local legal aid organizations** — Many offer free help with record expungement, drivers license restoration, and benefits appeals
- **State workforce development office** — Free job training and placement assistance

## A Final Word

You didn't survive what you survived to stumble at the gate. The system is not going to hold your hand through this — but it's also not going to stop you if you know what you're doing.

This checklist is a starting point. Use it. Then reach out.
