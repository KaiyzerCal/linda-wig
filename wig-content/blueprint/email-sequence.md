**Email 2 — The Cost of Performance**

Subject: What performance actually costs you
Preview text: Not the energy. The other thing.

---

You read the audit. Good.

I want to talk about one thing that runs through all ten points, because understanding it changes how you read everything else.

Performance is expensive.

Not just in energy — though it costs that too. It costs you something more fundamental: the ability to be known.

Here's what I mean.

When you've been performing long enough — managing your image, calibrating how you come across, being slightly different in every room — you get good at it. Genuinely good. People respect you. Some of them even love you.

But here's the part that doesn't get said: what they love is the performance. And somewhere in you, you know it.

That's the specific loneliness of a managed life. You can be surrounded by people and still feel fundamentally unseen, because the thing they're responding to isn't you. It's what you've built to represent you.

This isn't a moral failure. The performance developed for reasons. It kept you safe in places that required protection. It got you things you needed. It's not the enemy.

But there's a point where it stops serving you and starts costing you more than it gives back.

Most men hit that point somewhere between 30 and 45. They've built the life, checked the boxes, and there's still something hollow at the center that the next achievement doesn't fill.

That hollowness is not a mystery. It's the distance between who they are and who they've been presenting.

Closing that gap is the work.

More on that Thursday.

— Bishop
