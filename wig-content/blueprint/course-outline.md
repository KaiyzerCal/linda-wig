## Program Overview

The Interpersonal Blueprint is not a lecture series. It is a structured program built for men who are ready to examine — and deliberately reconstruct — the patterns that are shaping their relationships, their identity, and the quality of their daily lives.

Twelve modules. One per week. Each module includes content, a workbook exercise, and a reflection prompt. The work is designed to compound — each module builds on the previous one.

**Who this is for:** Men 28–55 who sense that something in how they operate isn't working — in relationships, in their sense of self, or in the gap between who they're performing to be and who they actually are.

**Who this is not for:** Men looking for quick tips, dating strategies, or techniques. This program does not teach tactics. It teaches understanding — of yourself, your patterns, and the men you've been shaped to be.

## Module Structure (Each Module)

- **Title and Learning Objective**
- **Key Content Points** (3–5 bullets)
- **Workbook Exercise** (a structured writing or reflection task)
- **Reflection Prompt** (one question to sit with through the week)

## MODULE 1: Taking Inventory

**Learning Objective:** Establish an honest baseline — where you actually are, not where you'd like to be.

**Key Content Points:**
- Why honest self-assessment is the most difficult and most necessary first step
- The difference between your stated identity and your revealed identity (the gap between who you say you are and how you actually behave)
- The primary life domains: relationships, career, physical health, emotional life, purpose — and how to evaluate each without performance
- What "done performing" actually means as a starting point
- Setting the terms for twelve weeks: what you will and won't do, what you're willing to look at

**Workbook Exercise:** Life Domains Inventory. Rate each of the five primary life domains on two scales: (1) where you actually are and (2) where you are performing to be. For each domain where there's a gap, write one paragraph about what's underneath the gap.

**Reflection Prompt:** *Where in your life are you expending the most energy on management — and what would happen if you stopped managing it?*

---

## MODULE 2: The Inherited Self

**Learning Objective:** Identify the beliefs, patterns, and definitions of manhood that were handed to you before you could choose them.

**Key Content Points:**
- How identity is shaped by family environment, particularly fathers and father-figures
- The specific lessons men learn about strength, emotion, money, women, and worth before age 12
- How inherited beliefs operate as invisible instructions decades after their source is gone
- The difference between inheriting patterns and being defined by them
- The work of choosing versus defaulting

**Workbook Exercise:** The Father Map. Write about your father (or primary male figure) in four categories: what he taught you explicitly about being a man; what he demonstrated through his behavior; what he failed to show you; what you decided about manhood based on watching him. No judgment — observation.

**Reflection Prompt:** *What did you decide about yourself as a child that you're still acting on as if it were fact?*

---

## MODULE 3: The Performance History

**Learning Objective:** Trace the origins and evolution of your personal performance — the curated self you've presented to the world.

**Key Content Points:**
- When and why the performance began — the specific circumstances that made self-presentation feel necessary
- How the performance has evolved across contexts: home, work, relationships, friendship
- What the performance protects — and what it costs
- The exhaustion of chronic self-management
- What moments of genuine authenticity look like and what they have in common

**Workbook Exercise:** Performance Timeline. Map the development of your performed self across your life in three phases: early formation (before 18), consolidation (18–30), and operation (30–now). For each phase, identify the key performance you adopted and what you were protecting.

**Reflection Prompt:** *Who are the people in your life who know the real version of you — and what allowed you to be that version with them?*

---

## MODULE 4: Presence — The Foundational Practice

**Learning Objective:** Understand and begin developing the practice of genuine presence — being fully in your life rather than managing it from a distance.

**Key Content Points:**
- What presence is in practical terms: full attention, internal stillness, absence of performance
- Why most men are chronically absent even when physically present
- The relationship between distraction, anxiety, and absence
- Presence as a practice, not a personality trait — what it requires daily
- The specific impact of increased presence on relationships, decision-making, and self-awareness

**Workbook Exercise:** Presence Mapping. For one full week before continuing, track your presence in three specific contexts: (1) with your most important relationship, (2) in your work, (3) alone. Note when you're present, when you've drifted, and what pulled you away.

**Reflection Prompt:** *What are you avoiding when you're most absent — and what would it mean to stay?*

---

## MODULE 5: Energy Awareness

**Learning Objective:** Develop the capacity to observe and understand your own energy — what you broadcast and how it affects the people around you.

**Key Content Points:**
- The practical meaning of energy in interpersonal contexts: the felt quality of a person
- Reading your own energy: contracted versus expansive, anxious versus grounded, defended versus open
- How anxiety, stress, and unprocessed emotion manifest in your energy signal
- The relationship between internal state and external broadcast
- Beginning to consciously calibrate your energy — not performing, but regulating

**Workbook Exercise:** Energy Audit. For three days, check in four times daily (morning, midday, afternoon, evening) and note your energy state in one word. At the end of three days, identify patterns: When is your energy most contracted? What precedes it? When are you most grounded?

**Reflection Prompt:** *If the five people closest to you described your default energy in one word each, what would they say?*

---

## MODULE 6: Identity Architecture

**Learning Objective:** Distinguish between identity anchored in roles and circumstances versus identity anchored in values and character.

**Key Content Points:**
- The vulnerability of externally-anchored identity: it moves with circumstance
- Identifying your current identity anchors (job, relationship, status, reputation)
- The destabilizing events that reveal your foundation — and what they tell you about where you're built
- Core values as stable identity architecture
- The difference between your stated values and your operative values — the values you actually act from

**Workbook Exercise:** Values Excavation. List your top ten stated values. Then, for each one, identify a specific decision or behavior from the past six months that reflects that value — or one that contradicts it. Revise your list to reflect what your behavior actually reveals, not what you wish it revealed.

**Reflection Prompt:** *If your character were defined only by your observable choices over the past year, what would it say you stand for?*

---

## MODULE 7: Emotional Intelligence — The Missing Piece

**Learning Objective:** Develop a working understanding of emotional intelligence as a practical capacity, not a therapeutic concept.

**Key Content Points:**
- Emotional intelligence is not emotional expressiveness — it is the ability to navigate emotional experience without being controlled by it
- The four emotional capacities most men are underdeveloped in: recognition, regulation, expression, and reception
- How emotional suppression manifests — explosions, coldness, distance, physical symptoms
- The relationship between emotional intelligence and relational authority
- What emotional range looks like in a man and why it is more attractive, not less

**Workbook Exercise:** Emotional Vocabulary Expansion. Most men operate with a vocabulary of three or four emotions (angry, fine, tired, stressed). This week, practice naming what you're actually feeling with more precision. Use a provided 50-word emotional vocabulary list. Journal for seven days — every evening, name three distinct emotions you experienced that day.

**Reflection Prompt:** *What emotion do you have the hardest time acknowledging — and what would it mean to let someone else see it?*

---

## MODULE 8: Conflict and Character

**Learning Objective:** Understand your conflict patterns and develop the capacity to navigate disagreement from security rather than threat.

**Key Content Points:**
- The three primary male conflict responses: attack, withdrawal, capitulation
- What each response communicates and what drives it
- The specific emotional trigger beneath most conflict: the experience of threat to identity or worth
- What it looks like to receive challenge without being destabilized
- Conflict as information — how to use it rather than survive it

**Workbook Exercise:** Conflict Autopsy. Select three significant conflicts from the past year. For each one, write: (1) what triggered you, (2) what your response was, (3) what need underneath the conflict you were protecting, (4) what response you would choose now with more awareness.

**Reflection Prompt:** *What does conflict reveal about where you feel most threatened?*

---

## MODULE 9: Desire, Standards, and Self-Respect

**Learning Objective:** Develop clarity about what you want and the self-respect required to maintain standards that reflect it.

**Key Content Points:**
- Desire in a complete sense: what you want in relationships, in work, in life — named clearly
- The relationship between self-respect and the standards you maintain
- How men abandon their standards under pressure — for approval, for peace, for fear
- Standards are not demands — they are expressions of what you will and won't participate in
- The attractive quality of a man who knows what he wants and won't accept what he doesn't

**Workbook Exercise:** Desire Inventory and Standards Audit. In three areas — romantic relationship, professional life, personal life — write out what you actually want (not what you think you should want). Then write out one standard in each area that you have been failing to maintain, and write the specific action you will take this week to reinstate it.

**Reflection Prompt:** *Where are you settling — and what is it costing you?*

---

## MODULE 10: Relationships — What You're Actually Building

**Learning Objective:** Examine the pattern of your most important relationships and understand what you have been building versus what you intended.

**Key Content Points:**
- Relationships reflect the man's interior — your relational patterns are a mirror
- The difference between relationships that affirm your performance and relationships that know you
- Attachment patterns and how they operate in adult relationships
- What it costs a relationship when the man is present in body but absent in attention and vulnerability
- The specific practices that deepen relationship: full attention, honest expression, repair after rupture

**Workbook Exercise:** Relationship Quality Assessment. For your three most important relationships (can include partner, close friend, child, parent), write an honest assessment of: (1) how known you are in this relationship, (2) how present you are, (3) what pattern keeps this relationship from going deeper, (4) one change you could make this week.

**Reflection Prompt:** *What would the most important relationship in your life look like if you brought everything you've learned in this program into it?*

---

## MODULE 11: The Mission

**Learning Objective:** Identify or clarify the purpose that anchors your life and understand its relationship to your identity, your energy, and your gravity.

**Key Content Points:**
- Purpose as identity anchor — the thing you're building that is larger than your daily circumstances
- The difference between a mission and a job or role
- Why men without a clear mission tend to be more easily destabilized, more approval-seeking, more prone to distraction
- How your mission shapes your energy, your standards, and the quality of people you attract
- Finding or reclarifying your mission — the questions that surface it

**Workbook Exercise:** Mission Excavation. Answer these three questions in writing, with as much honesty and specificity as you can: (1) What problem in the world do you feel most called to address? (2) What would you spend your time on if money were not a constraint? (3) What do you want to have built by the end of your life? Use the overlap between the answers to draft a one-sentence mission statement.

**Reflection Prompt:** *Are the choices you're making daily aligned with the man you want to be in ten years?*

---

## MODULE 12: Integration — The Ongoing Work

**Learning Objective:** Consolidate the learning from twelve weeks into a sustainable, integrated practice — not a destination, but a way of operating.

**Key Content Points:**
- Integration is not completion — it is the ongoing practice of applying what you know
- Reviewing the full arc: from inventory through mission
- The specific areas where each man tends to drift and how to notice the drift early
- Building accountability structures that sustain the work
- What a man looks like after a year of intentional interior development — not perfect, but whole

**Workbook Exercise:** Twelve-Week Integration Letter. Write a letter to yourself — from where you are now to where you intend to be in twelve months. Include: (1) the specific patterns you are committed to changing, (2) the values you are committing to act from, (3) the one relationship you are going to show up in differently, (4) your mission statement. Seal it and open it in twelve months.

**Reflection Prompt:** *What does it mean to you to be a man who is done performing — and what is the first thing that changes when you become that man?*
